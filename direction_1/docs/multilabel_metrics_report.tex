\section{Purpose}

This report explains the multilabel metrics in
\texttt{outputs/direction\_1\_run/multilabel\_metrics\_aggregated.csv}.
Multilabel prediction differs from ordinary classification because each sample
can have more than one correct label. A prediction is therefore a vector of
label probabilities or binary label decisions rather than a single class.

The aggregated file groups results by dataset, method, and training fraction.
The training fraction indicates how much of the training data was used, such as
\texttt{0.1}, \texttt{0.5}, or \texttt{1.0}. The \texttt{\_mean} columns are
averages across seeds, and the \texttt{\_std} columns measure variation across
seeds.

\section{Metric Definitions}

\begin{longtable}{p{0.23\linewidth} p{0.55\linewidth} p{0.14\linewidth}}
\toprule
\textbf{Metric} & \textbf{How it is calculated} & \textbf{Better value} \\
\midrule
\endhead

\texttt{micro\_f1}
&
Pools true positives, false positives, and false negatives across all labels,
then computes F1:
\[
\mathrm{micro\_F1} =
\frac{2TP}{2TP + FP + FN}.
\]
This measures overall label-level performance and is influenced most by common
labels.
&
Higher
\\
\addlinespace

\texttt{macro\_f1}
&
Computes F1 separately for each label and then averages equally over labels:
\[
\mathrm{macro\_F1} = \frac{1}{L}\sum_{\ell=1}^{L}\mathrm{F1}_{\ell}.
\]
This gives rare labels the same weight as frequent labels.
&
Higher
\\
\addlinespace

\texttt{example\_f1}
&
Computes F1 per sample by comparing the predicted label set with the true label
set, then averages over samples:
\[
\mathrm{example\_F1} =
\frac{1}{N}\sum_i
\frac{2|Y_i \cap \hat{Y}_i|}{|Y_i| + |\hat{Y}_i|}.
\]
&
Higher
\\
\addlinespace

\texttt{hamming\_loss}
&
Measures the fraction of individual label decisions that are wrong:
\[
\mathrm{hamming\_loss} =
\frac{1}{NL}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\sum_{\ell=1}^{L}
\mathbf{1}[y_{i\ell} \ne \hat{y}_{i\ell}].
\]
&
Lower
\\
\addlinespace

\texttt{average\_precision}
&
Uses predicted label scores rather than only binary decisions. It measures how
well relevant labels are ranked ahead of irrelevant labels, using the area under
the precision-recall curve averaged over labels.
&
Higher
\\
\addlinespace

\texttt{label\_ranking\_loss}
&
Measures how often an irrelevant label is ranked above a relevant label:
\[
\frac{1}{N}\sum_i
\frac{
\#\{(\ell_a,\ell_b): y_{i\ell_a}=1, y_{i\ell_b}=0,
s_{i\ell_a} \le s_{i\ell_b}\}
}{
\#\{(\ell_a,\ell_b): y_{i\ell_a}=1, y_{i\ell_b}=0\}
}.
\]
Here $s_{i\ell}$ is the predicted score for label $\ell$.
&
Lower
\\
\addlinespace

\texttt{subset\_accuracy}
&
Strict exact-match accuracy. A sample is counted as correct only when every
label decision exactly matches the true label vector:
\[
\frac{1}{N}\sum_i \mathbf{1}[Y_i = \hat{Y}_i].
\]
This is usually harsh for multilabel prediction.
&
Higher
\\
\addlinespace

\texttt{hamming\_accuracy}
&
The complement of hamming loss:
\[
\mathrm{hamming\_accuracy} = 1 - \mathrm{hamming\_loss}.
\]
&
Higher
\\
\addlinespace

\texttt{precision\_at\_1}, \texttt{precision\_at\_3}, \texttt{precision\_at\_5}
&
Sort labels by predicted score and check how many of the top $k$ labels are
actually relevant:
\[
P@k = \frac{1}{N}\sum_i
\frac{|Y_i \cap \operatorname{top}_k(s_i)|}{k}.
\]
&
Higher
\\
\addlinespace

\texttt{elapsed\_seconds}
&
Runtime for training the multilabel method and evaluating it:
\[
t_{\mathrm{elapsed}} = t_{\mathrm{finish}} - t_{\mathrm{start}}.
\]
&
Lower
\\

\bottomrule
\end{longtable}

\section{Interpretation}

On \texttt{synthetic\_image\_multilabel}, \texttt{one\_vs\_rest\_logistic}
dominates all reported predictive metrics at all training fractions and is also
the fastest method. This indicates that the generated image labels are linearly
separable enough for the simple baseline to solve the task.

On \texttt{synthetic\_multilabel}, the best method depends on the metric and
training fraction. At low training fraction, \texttt{one\_vs\_rest\_logistic}
has stronger F1 and top-1 precision, while \texttt{mlp\_multilabel} has better
hamming loss, ranking, subset accuracy, and top-3/top-5 precision. At 50\% and
100\% training data, \texttt{mlp\_multilabel} wins most predictive metrics, but
\texttt{one\_vs\_rest\_logistic} remains fastest and wins some ranking/top-k
metrics.
